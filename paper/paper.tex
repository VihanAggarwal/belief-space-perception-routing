\begin{abstract}
Reliable perception under deadline constraints must tolerate two
co-occurring failure modes that are rarely modeled jointly: sensor
faults and compute contention. When a sensing channel degrades and
onboard compute is simultaneously constrained, naive or decoupled
routing compounds errors---committing to a compute-heavy inference
config exactly when it can least afford to execute. We present a
\textbf{belief-space perception router} that maintains separate
temporally-smoothed beliefs over persistent sensor-fault state and
compute-contention state and \emph{couples} them at each timestep
to condition config selection, selecting from a four-point
accuracy--latency frontier (YOLO11x@1280, YOLO11x@640,
YOLO11n@1280, YOLO11n@640) via a noisy-OR coupling term whose
coefficient is fit on a held-out calibration draw. We evaluate on
\textbf{three real datasets spanning two camera geometries}:
TartanDrive (off-road, rectilinear), RADIATE (adverse weather---
rain, snow, fog, night---rectilinear stereo), and WoodScape
Soiling (lens contamination, fisheye), plus controlled injection
for tracker validation, with five seeds and 95\% confidence
intervals. The joint policy reduces deadline-miss rate by
$1.1$--$9.4$ percentage points wherever fault--contention coupling
is real, statistically significant in five of six coupled
conditions (a high-degradation off-road segment, rain, fog, night,
soiling; e.g.\ fog $+9.4$\,pp, 95\% CI $[7.1, 11.7]$; off-road
$+3.0$\,pp), while \emph{every} uncoupled control yields exactly
$0.0$\,pp---isolating the coupling, within this imposed regime, as
the driver of the gain; on the fault-free full off-road trajectory
the method is correctly inert ($0.0$\,pp). A simple belief threshold
performs similarly to the noisy-OR (no statistically detectable
difference), so the contribution is the coupling itself, not its
functional form. Persistence-aware belief reduces
config-switching for slow degradations but not fast-flickering rain,
a fault-type-dependent effect. Experiments ran on Apple~M5 (Metal); the
deadline self-calibrates per device, so the deadline-miss
\emph{comparison}---not absolute latency---is the reported result.
All code and outputs are released for reproducibility.%
\footnote{Repository: \url{https://github.com/VihanAggarwal/belief-space-perception-routing}}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Autonomous robots deployed in unstructured environments---off-road
vehicles, planetary rovers, warehouse mobile bases---face two
distinct and practically coupled failure modes that challenge
perception pipelines. First, sensor channels degrade: cameras are
occluded by mud or rain droplets, washed out at night, or fogged;
IMUs saturate under impact shock; LiDAR returns drop in fog.
Second, onboard compute is contested: a single edge processor must
concurrently service perception, planning, and control threads, so
peak load directly compresses the time available for any one
inference call.

These stressors are not independent. Aggressive terrain traversal
that occludes the camera is precisely the regime that also spikes
motor-control CPU load; rain that contaminates the lens also raises
frame-to-frame processing as segmentation outputs densify. Treating
fault and contention as independent signals lets a router select a
compute-heavy inference config at the moment it can least afford to
execute it, or trust a degraded channel because no fault signal
arrived within the tight latency window.

Existing work addresses each stressor in isolation. Fault-tolerant
fusion down-weights degraded modalities
\cite{antonante2022monitoring, tian2025fault}; compute-aware
routers select inference paths from an available budget
\cite{laskaridis2021adaptive, teerapittayanon2016branchynet}. No
prior work, to our knowledge, estimates both a sensor-fault belief
and a compute-contention belief and exploits their coupling to
reduce deadline-miss rate.

This paper makes four contributions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item We formulate the coupled sensor-fault / compute-contention
    belief state using per-channel switching HMMs and a two-state
    compute HMM, fit from observable signals without task-specific
    labels (\S\ref{sec:method}).
  \item We derive a noisy-OR joint policy that fuses compute belief
    with a coupling prediction from the sensor belief; the coupling
    coefficient $\kappa$ is fit on a held-out calibration draw to
    prevent leakage (\S\ref{sec:method}).
  \item We evaluate across three real datasets spanning two camera
    geometries---off-road (TartanDrive), adverse weather (RADIATE:
    rain/snow/fog/night), and lens soiling on a fisheye camera
    (WoodScape)---plus controlled injection, with five seeds and
    95\% $t$-interval CIs, reporting negative and null findings
    plainly (\S\ref{sec:experiments}).
  \item We release a fully reproducible, config-driven Python
    pipeline covering data acquisition, belief estimation, routing
    simulation, and statistical testing.\footnotemark[1]
\end{itemize}

The central finding (Fig.~\ref{fig:summary}) is that modeling the
coupling between sensor fault and compute contention produces a
reduction in deadline-miss rate that ranges from small (rain
$+1.1$\,pp, soiling $+1.6$\,pp) through moderate (off-road segment
$+3.0$\,pp) to large (night $+5.9$\,pp, fog $+9.4$\,pp). The
condition-level pattern suggests the benefit may track
deadline-binding degradation \emph{severity} more closely than fault
frequency or correlation strength (an interpretation, not a
directly measured variable; \S\ref{sec:discussion}). The effect is
present only where coupling is present and \emph{exactly zero} in
every uncoupled control, establishing that exploiting the
coupling---not the belief machinery itself---drives the gain;
whether field deployments exhibit such fault--contention coupling is
a separate empirical question we scope in \S\ref{sec:discussion}.
The effect also vanishes harmlessly on a clean trajectory with no
faults.
Persistence-aware belief tracking helps on slow degradations but
hurts on fast-flickering rain, a fault-type-dependent result we
report without qualification.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Off-Road and Adverse-Weather Datasets}

TartanDrive \cite{triest2022tartandrive} is a large-scale
multi-modal off-road driving dataset collected from a modified
Yamaha Viking ATV across diverse terrains including tall grass,
rocks, and mud, providing seven sensing modalities across roughly
200{,}000 driving interactions. TartanDrive~2.0
\cite{sivaprakasam2024tartandrive2} extended this with additional
LiDAR and infrastructure for self-supervised learning research.
RADIATE \cite{sheeny2021radiate} provides adverse-weather driving
sequences (rain, snow, fog, night) from a Heriot-Watt vehicle
platform including stereo ZED cameras, making it well suited for
real sensor-degradation evaluation under controlled weather
conditions. WoodScape \cite{yogamani2019woodscape} provides
fisheye automotive images including soiling annotations, enabling
cross-optics generalization with real lens-contamination ground
truth.

\subsection{Sensor Fault Detection and Fault-Tolerant Fusion}

Probabilistic fault monitoring \cite{antonante2022monitoring}
provides a unified framework for deterministic, probabilistic, and
learning-based fault identification in perception pipelines.
Anomaly-detection surveys \cite{bogdoll2022anomaly} review
out-of-distribution approaches including those applicable to
sensor-level failure. Hou et al.\ \cite{hou2023fault} apply
information fusion to autonomous-driving perception fault
diagnosis. Recent stress-testing of multi-sensor fusion systems
\cite{tian2025fault} shows significant accuracy collapse under
individual-stream corruption in production-grade systems. The
RoboDrive Challenge \cite{robodrive2024} benchmarked perception
robustness across sensor malfunctions, adverse weather, and BEV
detection at scale. Our work differs from all of these by
maintaining a continuous, temporally smoothed fault belief rather
than a binary flag, and by coupling that belief to the compute
state.

\subsection{Compute-Aware and Adaptive Inference}

BranchyNet \cite{teerapittayanon2016branchynet} introduced
multi-exit inference allowing early termination at intermediate
layers. Laskaridis et al.\ \cite{laskaridis2021adaptive} survey
design challenges for early-exit networks across edge-deployment
settings. Wang et al.\ \cite{wang2021notallimages} extend adaptive
inference to token-level dynamic transformers. In autonomous
driving, AD-EE \cite{adee2025} and Nav-EE \cite{navee2025} apply
early exiting to vision-language perception and navigation models;
Temporal Patience \cite{temporalpatience2023} explores adaptive
exit for embedded radar processing. These works treat the compute
budget as a deployment-time constant or a per-frame observable; we
treat it as a latent runtime state that must be estimated jointly
with sensor fault.

\subsection{Joint Fault and Resource Modeling}

PRAM-R \cite{pramr2025} is the closest concurrent work, routing
modalities via an LLM-guided reliability-aware mechanism, but does
not estimate compute contention or model cross-modal coupling with
a fault belief. The adaptive fusion of Mees et al.\
\cite{mees2016choosing} and DynMM \cite{dynmm} learn modality
gating via expert weighting or uncertainty cues but assume all
sensors are available and do not model compute. To our knowledge,
no prior work couples a temporally-smoothed sensor-fault belief to
a compute-contention belief and uses their empirical coupling to
improve deadline-constrained routing.

\subsection{Inference Config Frontier}

We define a discrete set of four inference configurations ordered
by accuracy--latency tradeoff:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{C1}: YOLO11x @ imgsz 1280 (highest accuracy,
    reference and pseudo-GT)
  \item \textbf{C2}: YOLO11x @ imgsz 640
  \item \textbf{C3}: YOLO11n @ imgsz 1280
  \item \textbf{C4}: YOLO11n @ imgsz 640
\end{itemize}

A precision axis (fp16) is defined for CUDA but collapses to fp32
on the Metal (MPS) device used here; this is disclosed and is not a
reported axis. Task accuracy for any config is the IoU-matched,
per-class detection-agreement F1 with C1 on the same frame
(pseudo-GT): agreement-with-reference, not external ground truth.
Bias from systematic C1 errors is acknowledged throughout. The
inference deadline is set as the median end-to-end latency of C1
under nominal compute, so C1 is borderline-feasible uncontended and
infeasible under contention while cheaper configs remain feasible.
This creates real routing pressure without hand-tuning the
deadline, and it makes the deadline-miss \emph{comparison}
device-agnostic.

\subsection{Sensor Fault Belief}

We model per-channel fault state with a 4-state switching HMM:
latent states $z \in \{\text{nominal}, \text{degrading},
\text{faulted}, \text{recovering}\}$, Gaussian emissions per state,
and a transition matrix fit from labeled fault segments. The
observable health signal $h_k^t$ per channel $k$ is computed from
the raw frame:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Blur}: variance of the Laplacian of the image
    (polar-unwrapped before the Laplacian for the fisheye track, so
    radial distortion is not read as blur).
  \item \textbf{Illumination}: grayscale histogram entropy.
  \item \textbf{Occlusion}: sparse optical-flow feature-track
    survival rate, or---on the fisheye soiling track, where optical
    flow on the raw fisheye is invalid---the \emph{real
    soiling-mask} pixel fraction (mask-based ground truth).
\end{itemize}

Each signal is robust-$z$ normalized on an EMA-smoothed baseline,
thresholded, and smoothed with minimum-duration and asymmetric
recovery hysteresis to produce per-channel fault labels for HMM
fitting. Forward filtering gives posterior belief
$b_{f,k}^t = P(z_k^t = \text{faulted} \mid o_{1:t})$, and the
aggregate sensor fault belief is $b_f^t = \max_k\, b_{f,k}^t$.
Fig.~\ref{fig:belief} shows the occlusion belief tracking real
degradation on a RADIATE rain sequence.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig_belief_occlusion.png}
  \caption{Occlusion fault belief on real RADIATE rain. The raw
  health signal (track-survival) is noisy; the switching-HMM
  posterior $P(\text{faulted})$ recovers sustained degradation
  episodes. Balanced accuracy up to $0.95$ on real occlusion.}
  \label{fig:belief}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Compute Contention Belief}

Compute state is modeled with a 2-state HMM
$z_c \in \{\text{nominal}, \text{contended}\}$ with Gaussian
emissions over the feature vector
$[\text{p95},\, \text{p99},\, \text{queue depth}]^\top$ of recent
latencies. Real contention is induced during evaluation with
device-appropriate load generation: CUDA kernel threads on
NVIDIA GPUs, CPU stress on CPU-only devices, and---on Apple
silicon---a separate \emph{competitor process} (Metal command
buffers are not thread-safe, so the co-running GPU workload cannot
share the inference thread). The induced load produces a
$\approx\!2.5\times$ p95 latency shift between nominal and
contended states (Fig.~\ref{fig:contention}). Forward filtering
gives $b_c^t = P(z_c^t = \text{contended} \mid \ell_{1:t})$.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig_contention.png}
  \caption{Per-frame latency under nominal vs.\ contended compute
  (RADIATE rain). The induced contention shifts the p95 latency by
  $\approx\!2.5\times$, pushing the reference config C1 past the
  self-calibrated deadline while cheaper configs remain feasible.}
  \label{fig:contention}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Coupled Policy and Coupling}

Fig.~\ref{fig:overview} summarizes the architecture. We emphasize
that the method estimates the two marginal beliefs $b_f^t$ and
$b_c^t$ independently and \emph{couples} them at decision time via
the noisy-OR term below; it does not estimate a full joint
posterior $P(z_f^t, z_c^t \mid o_{1:t})$. A factorial or
cross-coupled HMM that maintains the joint posterior is a natural
upgrade (\S\ref{sec:discussion}); we adopt the lighter coupled-fusion
form because it is parameter-frugal and isolates the single
coupling coefficient $\kappa$ as the object of study. Both the
coupling-aware policy and the decoupled baseline select, from the
configs feasible under the predicted compute state, the one with
maximum expected task accuracy. A config is feasible in state $s$
if its median latency in $s$ meets the deadline. The two policies
differ only in how the contention probability $p_{\text{cont}}^t$
is formed:

\textbf{Decoupled}: $p_{\text{cont}}^t = b_c^t$ (compute belief
alone).

\textbf{Joint} (noisy-OR):
\begin{equation}
  p_{\text{cont}}^t = 1 - (1 - b_c^t)\,(1 - \kappa\, b_f^t)
  \label{eq:noisyor}
\end{equation}
where $\kappa \in [0,1]$ is the coupling coefficient fit on a
held-out calibration draw from the same trace (never the test
window, to prevent leakage; fitted $\kappa \approx 0.80$ across
tracks). This models the empirical observation that sensor-fault
onset correlates with compute-contention onset in coupled regimes
($r = 0.31$--$0.76$) but not in uncoupled periodic schedules
($r \approx -0.12$--$0.01$).

The decoupled baseline uses both sensor and compute signals (the
sensor belief still drives accuracy weighting); it simply never
forms the cross-prediction. The entire RQ-H delta is therefore
attributable to the coupling term in Eq.~\eqref{eq:noisyor} alone,
making it a fair baseline. Abstention (withholding a detection when
belief confidence is low) is excluded from the headline comparison
to prevent differential abstention rates from confounding
deadline-miss, and is evaluated separately against an oracle.

\subsection{Evaluation Policies}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{CoupledPolicy} (``joint''): noisy-OR coupling
    (Eq.~\eqref{eq:noisyor}), $\kappa$ fit on calibration draw.
  \item \textbf{DecoupledPolicy}: compute belief only, same
    feasibility set.
  \item \textbf{ThresholdPolicy}: rule-based coupling
    $p_{\text{cont}}=\max(b_c,\mathbf{1}[b_f>0.5])$.
  \item \textbf{MemorylessPolicy}: per-frame threshold on raw
    signals, no HMM, no coupling.
  \item \textbf{Hysteresis (model-derived)}: dwell time set from
    the HMM's expected false-transition rate.
  \item \textbf{Hysteresis (fixed)}: hand-tuned dwell time.
  \item \textbf{Oracle}: routes with ground-truth fault and
    compute state.
\end{itemize}

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Dataset Tracks}

\textbf{Track A --- TartanDrive (real off-road).} The full
\texttt{turnpike\_afternoon\_fall} trajectory: 1{,}198 real camera
frames (\texttt{/multisense/left/image\_rect\_color}, bgr8,
$1024\times544$) from the public CMU AirLab Swift store. We use it
two ways. (i) \emph{Full trajectory (negative control).} This
daytime, good-visibility sequence contains \emph{no} frames that
cross the per-channel fault threshold (fault fraction $0.0\%$), so
it functions as a negative control: a real trajectory with nothing
to couple to. (ii) \emph{High-degradation segment.} A contiguous
$309$-frame rough-terrain segment (the opening ${\approx}31$\,s)
does contain real camera degradation---bumps and occlusion
spikes---at fault fraction $30.4\%$ ($r=0.74$), letting us test the
method on genuine off-road faults. The two are reported separately
and neither is cherry-picked: the segment is one contiguous window,
and the full trajectory is the complete sequence. Contention p95
latency shift: $2.50\times$ (full), $1.71\times$ (segment).

\textbf{Track B --- Controlled Injection (validation).}
Realistic corruptions---Gaussian-PSF blur, gamma/exposure crush,
alpha-composited occluders---are overlaid on real TartanDrive
frames with known onset and duration. Used exclusively to validate
belief-tracker accuracy against a ground-truth fault timeline and
for the controlled RQ-A2 arrival experiment; no headline number
depends on Track B.

\textbf{Track C --- WoodScape Soiling (fisheye; cross-optics
generalization).} The full 4{,}000-image soiling \emph{train}
split (3{,}303 frames after burst collection). Fisheye frames are
undistorted to a centered rectilinear projection using Valeo's
vendored radial camera model before YOLO inference; the occlusion
channel uses \emph{real soiling-mask ground truth} (soiled-pixel
fraction per frame) rather than an optical-flow proxy, and blur is
computed after polar-unwrap. Fault fraction $8.1\%$; deadline
$341$\,ms; contention p95 shift $2.50\times$; coupled $r=0.56$.

\textbf{Track D --- RADIATE adverse weather (real; primary).}
The four sequences \texttt{rain\_4\_0}, \texttt{snow\_1\_0},
\texttt{fog\_6\_0}, \texttt{night\_1\_0} (left ZED camera,
rectified to \texttt{camera\_left\_rect}); $2{,}589$--$2{,}659$
frames each. RADIATE is registration-gated (academic email, CC
BY-NC-SA). Fault fractions and coupling strengths vary widely by
weather---rain $44.4\%$ ($r=0.76$), night $12.7\%$ ($r=0.68$),
snow $18.5\%$ ($r=0.69$), fog $1.9\%$ ($r=0.31$)---which lets us
test whether the routing benefit tracks fault frequency,
correlation strength, or fault severity. Contention p95 shifts:
$2.54$--$2.59\times$. Faults are predominantly occlusion (droplets,
snow) and illumination (fog, night). The contention schedule
distinguishes a \emph{coupled} regime (contention follows
fault onset) from an \emph{uncoupled} periodic control
(Fig.~\ref{fig:regimes}).

\subsection{Implementation Details}

All experiments run on an Apple~M5 (10-core, 16\,GB unified
memory) using the Metal (MPS) backend in fp32; the precision axis
is CUDA-only and is not reported. Absolute latencies are
dev-environment and are not reported as headline numbers; the
deadline self-calibrates per device (median C1 nominal latency, in
the range $211$--$341$\,ms here), making the RQ-H comparison
environment-agnostic. Configuration is fully driven by
\texttt{config.yaml}; nothing latency-dependent is hardcoded. All
comparisons use 5 seeds with 95\% $t$-interval CIs and paired
difference tests; per-seed sensor measurement noise is the source
of seed variance (the real frames are identical across seeds).

The effect size does \emph{not} track fault frequency or
correlation strength: fog has the lowest fault fraction
($1.9\%$) and the weakest coupling ($r=0.31$) yet the
\emph{largest} benefit ($+9.4$\,pp). The driver is fault
\emph{severity}---rare but severe fog illumination/occlusion
episodes make the reference config infeasible exactly when
contention strikes, so anticipating them pre-emptively avoids a
large block of misses; pervasive but mild rain droplets move the
needle less. This is the operationally important regime: routing
matters most when degradation is severe enough to bind the
deadline.

We note a genuine accuracy--timeliness tradeoff in the most
severe conditions. On rain, night, and soiling the joint and
decoupled policies have near-matched task accuracy
(e.g.\ rain $0.855$ vs.\ $0.857$); on fog the joint policy trades
task accuracy ($0.861$ vs.\ $0.954$) to cut misses by $9.4$\,pp,
i.e.\ it deliberately downshifts to feasible configs under
predicted contention. The router thus exposes a controllable
operating point rather than a free lunch; deadline-miss is the
primary metric because a late detection is unusable downstream.

\subsubsection{Deadline-Aware Utility}

Because deadline-miss rate alone can reward aggressive downshifting,
we also report a per-frame combined metric that charges for the
accuracy sacrifice: on-time utility
$U = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t} a_t \,\mathbf{1}[\ell_t \le D]$: each frame
contributes its accuracy if it meets the deadline and zero if late,
averaged over all $T$ frames. Unlike $\bar{a}(1{-}m)$, this
preserves the per-frame link between the chosen config's accuracy
and its latency.
Table~\ref{tab:utility} shows the joint policy's point estimate of
$U$ is nonnegative in all six conditions, significantly positive for
off-road, rain, and night (paired CIs exclude zero); the snow, fog,
and soiling gains are small and not statistically distinguishable
from zero. Fog is effectively break-even ($+0.07$\,pp), the
timeliness gain and accuracy cost cancelling in expected on-time
accuracy even though the deadline-miss reduction stays large. No
point estimate is negative, so coupling does not lower on-time
utility in any tested condition, and the deadline-miss reduction is
an additional benefit where late results are unusable.

\subsubsection{Is the Noisy-OR Necessary? A Threshold Baseline}

To test whether the specific noisy-OR form matters---or whether any
sensor-triggered anticipation suffices---we add a simple rule-based
baseline that downshifts whenever either belief is high:
$p_{\text{cont}}^t = \max(b_c^t,\,\mathbf{1}[b_f^t > 0.5])$, a hard
union in place of the soft noisy-OR. Table~\ref{tab:baseline} shows
this threshold rule performs \emph{similarly} to the noisy-OR joint
policy on every condition---no statistically detectable difference
(joint$-$threshold within $\pm0.5$\,pp, all CIs include zero)---while
both beat the decoupled baseline by the same margin. We do not claim
formal equivalence (an equivalence test at, e.g., a $\pm1$\,pp margin
is under-powered at $n{=}5$); the point is that we detect no
advantage for the soft form. The contribution is therefore the
\emph{coupling itself}---feeding sensor-fault belief into contention
anticipation---rather than the particular noisy-OR functional form;
a hand-set threshold is a similarly performing realization under the
tested conditions. The threshold
comparison shows only that the particular fusion \emph{function} is
not essential to RQ-H; both policies still consume the HMM-derived
beliefs $b_f, b_c$, and RQ-A1 separately evaluates the contribution
of temporal belief smoothing. We also train a small two-hidden-layer
MLP ($32$--$16$ units, ReLU; cross-entropy, Adam) mapping the same
five features the policies use---the three sensor-channel beliefs,
the compute belief, and the deadline---to a config, supervised by
per-frame oracle labels. Each track is split \emph{temporally}
(first $70\%$ of frames train, last $30\%$ held out, no frame
shared) and evaluated over five seeds with the same hysteresis as
the policies. On held-out frames the MLP is competitive but does not
consistently beat the belief policy (lower deadline-miss on three
tracks, higher on three), with high variance on these short
single-sequence traces; we report it as a baseline, not a
contribution. The takeaway is again that the value lies in the
coupled features---shared by both---not the routing function. This
similarity is stable across observation-noise levels (the
joint$-$threshold gap stays within $\pm1.2$\,pp, n.s., for noise
$\sigma$ up to $0.75$ on fog and rain), so soft fusion confers no
distinct advantage even under heavy sensor noise.

\subsubsection{Robustness: Bootstrap, Deadline, and Coupling Strength}

Two checks probe whether the headline survives beyond the seed CIs
and the single operating point. First, a moving-block bootstrap
(block $=50$ frames, $B=2000$) over the temporal axis, pooled across
seeds, confirms the deltas are not driven by a handful of frames:
all six conditions exclude zero---off-road $[0.9,5.5]$, rain
$[0.3,2.3]$, snow $[0.9,4.9]$, fog $[6.9,12.0]$, night $[3.3,8.8]$,
soiling $[1.2,2.0]$\,pp. (Snow, non-significant under the
conservative $n{=}5$ seed interval, becomes significant under
temporal resampling; we retain the seed interval as primary.) This
checks robustness to temporal structure within a sequence, not
generalization across sequences, which remains future work.

Second, sweeping the deadline on fog (Table~\ref{tab:deadline})
shows the benefit is operating-point dependent and peaks exactly
where the deadline binds the reference config. At much tighter
deadlines ($0.8$--$0.9\times$) every policy must downshift and no
misses occur; at looser deadlines ($1.1$--$1.2\times$) misses are
rare and the gap shrinks to $+0.7$\,pp (n.s.). The effect is thus
concentrated in the intended borderline-feasibility regime rather
than persisting across arbitrary deadline settings---which is
exactly the regime the deadline rule (median nominal C1 latency)
targets, but the dependence on the operating point is real and we do
not claim otherwise. We sweep fog as the strongest case;
per-condition sweeps remain future work.

\subsubsection{RQ-A2: Model-Derived vs.\ Fixed Dwell Time}

Under non-stationary fault arrival, model-derived hysteresis is
$\approx\!4\times$ more responsive than fixed hysteresis
(onset-to-reconfiguration lag $1.6$ vs.\ $6.6$ frames) but switches
$\approx\!30\%$ more (switch rate $0.045$ vs.\ $0.034$; paired
difference significant). This is a tradeoff, not dominance: the
appropriate choice depends on whether reconfiguration latency or
switching cost is the binding constraint. Under stationary arrival
the two are statistically indistinguishable, so the auto-tuned
dwell matches hand-tuning without manual calibration.

\subsubsection{Belief Tracker Validation (Track B)}

On controlled injection with a known fault timeline, the blur and
occlusion belief trackers recover the injected onsets at balanced
accuracy $0.85$--$0.89$ with $3$--$6$ frame detection lag. On real
data the occlusion channel reaches balanced accuracy up to $0.95$
(e.g.\ RADIATE rain, Fig.~\ref{fig:belief}). On the fault-free
Track A all channels correctly report no sustained faults (balanced
accuracy $\approx\!0.5$, zero detected segments), consistent with
the $0.0\%$ fault fraction and the null RQ-H result.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Interpreting the Effect Size}

The reduction in deadline-miss rate spans an order of magnitude
across conditions ($1.1$--$9.4$\,pp) and is statistically
significant in five of six coupled real conditions. The
credibility of the claim rests less on any single number than on
the triangulation: positive across three real datasets, two camera
geometries (rectilinear and fisheye), and multiple degradation
physics (off-road terrain, droplets, snow, fog, night, soiling);
\emph{exactly zero} in every uncoupled control; and harmlessly zero
on the fault-free full trajectory.
That pattern isolates the fault--contention coupling, within the
imposed regime, as the driver of the gain rather than an artifact of
the belief estimator or the metric. The most actionable insight is
that the benefit appears to scale with fault \emph{severity}
(deadline-bindingness), not fault frequency or correlation
strength---fog being the clearest case. We note that ``severity'' is
here an interpretation inferred from condition-level outcomes, not a
variable we measure directly; a controlled severity sweep would test
it explicitly. We also found (Table~\ref{tab:baseline}) that a
simple threshold rule performs comparably to the noisy-OR on RQ-H
(no statistically detectable difference), so the contribution is
coupling sensor belief into contention anticipation, not the
specific fusion form.

\subsection{When Persistence Helps and Hurts}

Persistence-aware belief uniformly improves detection balanced
accuracy and reduces config-switching for slow, sustained
degradations (fog, night, soiling), passing the full pre-specified
gate on night. It hurts only on rain, where high-frequency droplet
occlusion defeats the slow-onset assumption baked into the HMM
smoothing. Rather than a blanket negative, this argues for matching
the smoothing time-constant to the fault's temporal scale.

\subsection{Limitations}

The evaluation is offline and trace-driven with no
hardware-in-the-loop. Compute contention is non-thermal commodity
load with a two-state model; real embedded contention has richer
structure. The sensor-to-compute coupling is regime-imposed in the
coupled/uncoupled controls rather than measured from real
co-occurrence---this is exactly what licenses the causal reading
(the uncoupled arm is the falsification test), but a field study of
naturally co-occurring fault and contention remains future work.
The pseudo-GT (agreement-with-reference) is blind to systematic C1
errors, and the per-channel fault labels used to fit the sensor HMM
are derived from the same health signals the belief then filters, so
detection validation is partly internal---the controlled injection
of Track~B, with independent ground-truth onsets, mitigates but does
not fully remove this circularity. HMM calibration is shared across
all policies (so the RQ-H delta is unbiased) but absolute belief
calibration is optimistic. RADIATE frames are rectified left-camera
only; provided radar, LiDAR, and detection annotations are unused.
The WoodScape soiling temporal axis is dataset order (7--8 frame
bursts), not continuous video time. Critically, the five seeds reuse
identical frames and vary only injected sensor-measurement noise, so
the reported CIs measure sensitivity to that noise---not
generalization across sequences, vehicles, or contention traces. A
within-sequence temporal-block bootstrap (\S\ref{sec:experiments})
confirms robustness to temporal resampling, but honest
cross-environment intervals still require bootstrap over independent
trajectories (future work), and the modest $n{=}5$ is why snow is
under-powered under the primary seed interval. Absolute latencies are
dev-environment (Apple~M5/MPS) and are not reported as headline
numbers.

\subsection{Future Work}

Three directions remain.
\emph{(i) Full joint posterior.} Replace the noisy-OR coupling with
a factorial or cross-coupled HMM that maintains
$P(z_f^t, z_c^t \mid o_{1:t})$, upgrading the contribution from
coupled belief fusion to true joint belief-state estimation.
\emph{(ii) Learned baselines.} Beyond the decoupled ablation, the
threshold rule (Table~\ref{tab:baseline}), and the small MLP router
(\S\ref{sec:experiments}, competitive but inconsistent), a
properly-tuned learned router or contextual bandit over
$(b_f, b_c)$, and an oracle that knows the coupling schedule, would
bound how much a learned fusion could add. \emph{(iii) Generalization and realism.} The
remaining sensitivity axes (contention intensity, fault--contention
delay, HMM window/transition parameters) should be swept as we did
for deadline and $\kappa$; and---most importantly---more independent
sequences with bootstrap over trajectories (beyond the
within-sequence temporal-block bootstrap already reported) for
cross-environment CIs, reportable datacenter-GPU latencies, real
embedded contention on a Jetson to replace the regime-imposed
coupling with measured co-occurrence, real RADIATE detection
annotations instead of pseudo-GT, and a closed-loop downstream-task
evaluation (e.g.\ MuJoCo navigation with rendered fault injection)
measuring task success rather than detector agreement.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a belief-space perception router that estimates
sensor-fault and compute-contention beliefs via separate HMMs and
\emph{couples} them with a noisy-OR term to reduce deadline-miss
rate versus a fair decoupled baseline. Evaluated across three real datasets
spanning two camera geometries---off-road (TartanDrive), adverse
weather (RADIATE rain/snow/fog/night), and fisheye lens soiling
(WoodScape)---with five seeds and 95\% CIs, the joint policy
reduces deadline-miss rate by $1.1$--$9.4$\,pp wherever
fault--contention coupling is real, significantly in five of six
coupled conditions, while every uncoupled control and the
fault-free trajectory produce exactly zero improvement. A simple
belief-threshold rule performs comparably on RQ-H, locating the
contribution in the coupling rather than its functional form; the
condition-level results suggest the benefit may track
deadline-binding severity more than frequency; and persistence-aware
belief helps on slow degradations but hurts on fast-flickering
rain---a fault-type-dependent finding reported without
qualification. The
work establishes coupled belief-space modeling of fault and
contention as a tractable, measurable research direction, and
releases a fully reproducible pipeline.
