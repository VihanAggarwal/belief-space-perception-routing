\begin{abstract}
Mobile-robot perception has to satisfy two constraints at once: the
sensor stream must remain usable, and inference must finish before a
deadline. These constraints are usually studied separately. In the
field, they can fail together: the same rain, mud, fog, or rough
terrain that degrades the image stream can also coincide with higher
compute load. A router that ignores that link may choose an expensive
model exactly when there is least time to run it.
We model both quantities as latent state. A switching hidden Markov
model estimates per-channel sensor fault, a two-state HMM estimates
compute contention, and a single coupling term lets sensor-fault
belief anticipate contention before the latency trace fully moves.
At each frame, the router chooses the most accurate feasible
inference configuration.
We evaluate on three real datasets and two camera geometries:
TartanDrive off-road driving, RADIATE rain/snow/fog/night, and
WoodScape fisheye soiling, with five seeds and 95\% confidence
intervals. When faults and contention are coupled, the joint router
reduces deadline misses by $1.1$ to $9.4$ percentage points,
significant in five of six fault-bearing conditions. Fog is the
largest case: faults are rare, but severe enough to make the
reference model miss its deadline. In every matched independent
control the gain is exactly zero, and on a fault-free trajectory the
router makes no change. A hard threshold on sensor belief performs
about as well as the noisy-OR policy, which places the contribution
in the coupling signal rather than its functional form. Experiments
ran on an Apple~M5; since the deadline is calibrated per device, we
report relative deadline-miss changes rather than absolute latency.
Code and data are released.%
\footnote{Repository: \url{https://github.com/VihanAggarwal/belief-space-perception-routing}}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Robots operating outside clean laboratory conditions have to solve
two timing problems at once. They need a sensor stream that still
describes the scene, and they need perception results before the
planner or controller stops waiting. Either side can fail. A camera
can be splashed with mud, washed out at dusk, or buried in fog; an
IMU can saturate on impact; lidar returns can thin out in rain. At
the same time, the processor is shared by perception, planning, and
control, so a load spike in one thread reduces the time left for a
detection pass.

Those two failures are not always independent. The rough segment
that dirties a lens may also increase control load; a rainy frame
can be both visually degraded and harder to process. A router that
scores sensor health and compute load in isolation can therefore
make the wrong choice: it may choose a heavy model when the machine
is already about to miss its deadline, or it may keep trusting a
bad camera because the fault estimate has not yet crossed a hard
threshold.

Existing work usually addresses one side of the problem. Fault-aware
fusion down-weights degraded sensing channels
\cite{antonante2022monitoring, tian2025fault}, while adaptive
inference chooses cheaper execution paths under tight budgets
\cite{laskaridis2021adaptive, teerapittayanon2016branchynet}. The
missing case is the coupled one: a router that treats sensor fault
and compute contention as related latent states and uses that
relationship when it chooses an inference configuration.

This paper makes four contributions. First, we estimate
per-channel sensor-fault state with switching HMMs and compute
state with a two-state HMM, using observable signals rather than
task labels. Second, we add a noisy-OR decision rule with one
coupling coefficient, fit on held-out calibration data. Third, we
evaluate on three real datasets and two camera geometries:
TartanDrive off-road driving, RADIATE adverse weather, and
WoodScape fisheye soiling. The evaluation uses five seeds,
confidence intervals, matched uncoupled controls, and a fault-free
negative control. Fourth, we release a config-driven pipeline for
data loading, belief estimation, routing, and statistical analysis.

The main result is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:summary}. When sensor
fault and compute contention are coupled, modeling the link reduces
deadline misses. The size of the reduction follows how much the
degradation stresses the deadline, not simply how often faults
occur. Fog is the clearest example: only $1.9\%$ of frames are
faulted, but those episodes are severe enough that coupling saves
more than nine points. Rain faults nearly half the frames and saves
about one point. The controls are equally important. When we break
the correlation, the gain drops to exactly zero in every matched
control, and the full fault-free TartanDrive trajectory stays at
zero. We do not claim that all field robots naturally exhibit this
coupling; we show that, when the coupling is present, exploiting it
changes deadline behavior. We also find that temporal smoothing is
not universally helpful: it helps slow degradations such as fog and
night, but hurts the fast flicker of rain droplets.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Off-Road and Adverse-Weather Datasets}

TartanDrive \cite{triest2022tartandrive} is a multi-modal
off-road driving dataset collected with a modified Yamaha Viking
ATV over grass, rocks, mud, and other rough terrain. It includes
seven sensing modalities and roughly 200{,}000 driving interactions.
TartanDrive~2.0 \cite{sivaprakasam2024tartandrive2} adds LiDAR and
self-supervised learning infrastructure. RADIATE
\cite{sheeny2021radiate} contains rain, snow, fog, and night
driving from a Heriot-Watt vehicle platform with stereo ZED
cameras, which makes it a useful source of real weather-driven
sensor degradation. WoodScape \cite{yogamani2019woodscape}
contains fisheye automotive imagery with soiling annotations, giving
us a cross-optics test case with real lens-contamination labels.

\subsection{Sensor Fault Detection and Fault-Tolerant Fusion}

Probabilistic fault monitoring \cite{antonante2022monitoring}
covers deterministic, probabilistic, and learning-based fault
identification in perception pipelines. Anomaly-detection surveys
\cite{bogdoll2022anomaly} review out-of-distribution methods that
can be applied to sensor-level failure. Hou et al.\
\cite{hou2023fault} use information fusion for autonomous-driving
perception fault diagnosis. Stress tests of multi-sensor fusion
systems \cite{tian2025fault} report large accuracy drops under
single-stream corruption in production-grade stacks. The RoboDrive
Challenge \cite{robodrive2024} benchmarks perception under sensor
malfunctions, adverse weather, and BEV detection tasks. We use this
line of work differently: the fault signal is a continuous,
temporally filtered belief, and it is allowed to affect the compute
state used by the router.

\subsection{Compute-Aware and Adaptive Inference}

BranchyNet \cite{teerapittayanon2016branchynet} introduced
multi-exit inference, where a network can terminate at intermediate
layers. Laskaridis et al.\ \cite{laskaridis2021adaptive} survey
early-exit design issues for edge deployment. Wang et al.\
\cite{wang2021notallimages} extend adaptive inference to
token-level dynamic transformers. In autonomous-driving settings,
AD-EE \cite{adee2025} and Nav-EE \cite{navee2025} use early exits
for vision-language perception and navigation models, while
Temporal Patience \cite{temporalpatience2023} studies adaptive exit
for embedded radar. These methods adapt computation, but they do
not model runtime contention as a latent state tied to sensor
health.

\subsection{Joint Fault and Resource Modeling}

PRAM-R \cite{pramr2025} is the closest concurrent work: it routes
modalities with an LLM-guided reliability mechanism, but it does not
estimate compute contention or couple that state to fault belief.
The adaptive fusion method of Mees et al.\ \cite{mees2016choosing}
and DynMM \cite{dynmm} learn modality gating from expert weights or
uncertainty cues, while assuming the sensors are available and
leaving compute contention outside the model. Our focus is narrower
and more timing-specific: couple a smoothed sensor-fault belief to a
compute-contention belief and measure whether that coupling reduces
deadline misses.

\subsection{Inference Config Frontier}

We define a discrete set of four inference configurations ordered
by accuracy--latency tradeoff:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{C1}: YOLO11x @ imgsz 1280 (highest accuracy,
    reference and pseudo-GT)
  \item \textbf{C2}: YOLO11x @ imgsz 640
  \item \textbf{C3}: YOLO11n @ imgsz 1280
  \item \textbf{C4}: YOLO11n @ imgsz 640
\end{itemize}

A precision axis (fp16) exists for CUDA, but the Metal (MPS) device
used here runs fp32, so precision is not a reported axis. We measure
task accuracy as IoU-matched, per-class detection-agreement F1 with
C1 on the same frame. This is agreement with a reference detector,
not external ground truth, and systematic C1 errors remain a
limitation. The deadline is the median end-to-end latency of C1
under nominal compute. With that rule, C1 is borderline feasible
without contention and infeasible under contention, while cheaper
configs remain feasible. The deadline is therefore calibrated by the
device instead of hand-tuned for a desired miss rate.

\subsection{Sensor Fault Belief}

We model per-channel fault state with a 4-state switching HMM:
latent states $z \in \{\text{nominal}, \text{degrading},
\text{faulted}, \text{recovering}\}$, Gaussian emissions per state,
and a transition matrix fit from labeled fault segments. The
observable health signal $h_k^t$ per channel $k$ is computed from
the raw frame:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Blur}: variance of the Laplacian of the image
    (polar-unwrapped before the Laplacian for the fisheye track, so
    radial distortion is not read as blur).
  \item \textbf{Illumination}: grayscale histogram entropy.
  \item \textbf{Occlusion}: sparse optical-flow feature-track
    survival rate, or, on the fisheye soiling track where optical
    flow on the raw fisheye is invalid, the \emph{real
    soiling-mask} pixel fraction (mask-based ground truth).
\end{itemize}

Each signal is robust-$z$ normalized against an EMA-smoothed
baseline, thresholded, and smoothed with minimum-duration and
asymmetric recovery hysteresis. These filtered signals provide
per-channel fault labels for fitting the HMM. Forward filtering
then gives the posterior belief
$b_{f,k}^t = P(z_k^t = \text{faulted} \mid o_{1:t})$, and the
aggregate sensor fault belief is $b_f^t = \max_k\, b_{f,k}^t$.
Fig.~\ref{fig:belief} shows the occlusion belief tracking real
degradation on a RADIATE rain sequence.

\begin{figure}[t]

  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig_belief_occlusion.png}
  \caption{Occlusion fault belief on real RADIATE rain. The raw
  health signal (track-survival) is noisy; the switching-HMM
  posterior $P(\text{faulted})$ recovers sustained degradation

\subsection{Compute Contention Belief}

Compute state is modeled with a 2-state HMM
$z_c \in \{\text{nominal}, \text{contended}\}$ with Gaussian
emissions over the feature vector
$[\text{p95},\, \text{p99},\, \text{queue depth}]^\top$ of recent
latencies. During evaluation we induce contention with
device-appropriate load: CUDA kernel threads on NVIDIA GPUs, CPU
stress on CPU-only devices, and, on Apple silicon, a separate
\emph{competitor process}. Metal command buffers are not thread-safe,
so the co-running GPU workload cannot share the inference thread.
The induced load produces a
$\approx\!2.5\times$ p95 latency shift between nominal and
contended states (Fig.~\ref{fig:contention}). Forward filtering
gives $b_c^t = P(z_c^t = \text{contended} \mid \ell_{1:t})$.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig_contention.png}
  \caption{Per-frame latency under nominal vs.\ contended compute
  (RADIATE rain). The induced contention shifts the p95 latency by
  $\approx\!2.5\times$, pushing the reference config C1 past the
  self-calibrated deadline while cheaper configs remain feasible.}
  \label{fig:contention}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Coupled Policy and Coupling}

Fig.~\ref{fig:overview} summarizes the architecture. The method
estimates two marginal beliefs, $b_f^t$ and $b_c^t$, and couples
them only at decision time through the noisy-OR term below. It does
not estimate the full posterior
$P(z_f^t, z_c^t \mid o_{1:t})$. A factorial or cross-coupled HMM
would be the natural full-posterior version
(\S\ref{sec:discussion}); we use the lighter form because it keeps
the experiment centered on one coefficient, $\kappa$. Both the
coupled policy and the decoupled baseline choose the highest
expected-accuracy config among those feasible under the predicted
compute state. A config is feasible in state $s$ if its median
latency in $s$ meets the deadline. The policies differ only in how
they form the contention probability $p_{\text{cont}}^t$:

\textbf{Decoupled}: $p_{\text{cont}}^t = b_c^t$ (compute belief
alone).

\textbf{Joint} (noisy-OR):
\begin{equation}
  p_{\text{cont}}^t = 1 - (1 - b_c^t)\,(1 - \kappa\, b_f^t)
  \label{eq:noisyor}
\end{equation}
where $\kappa \in [0,1]$ is the coupling coefficient fit on a
held-out calibration draw from the same trace (never the test
window, to prevent leakage; fitted $\kappa \approx 0.80$ across
tracks). This models the empirical observation that sensor-fault
onset correlates with compute-contention onset in coupled regimes
($r = 0.31$--$0.76$) but not in uncoupled periodic schedules
($r \approx -0.12$--$0.01$).

The decoupled baseline uses both sensor and compute signals (the
sensor belief still drives accuracy weighting); it simply never
forms the cross-prediction. The entire RQ-H delta is therefore
attributable to the coupling term in Eq.~\eqref{eq:noisyor} alone,
making it a fair baseline. Abstention (withholding a detection when
belief confidence is low) is excluded from the headline comparison
so different abstention rates cannot confound deadline-miss; we
evaluate abstention separately against an oracle.

\subsection{Evaluation Policies}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{CoupledPolicy} (``joint''): noisy-OR coupling
    (Eq.~\eqref{eq:noisyor}), $\kappa$ fit on calibration draw.
  \item \textbf{DecoupledPolicy}: compute belief only, same
    feasibility set.
  \item \textbf{ThresholdPolicy}: rule-based coupling
    $p_{\text{cont}}=\max(b_c,\mathbf{1}[b_f>0.5])$.
  \item \textbf{MemorylessPolicy}: per-frame threshold on raw
    signals, no HMM, no coupling.
  \item \textbf{Hysteresis (model-derived)}: dwell time set from
    the HMM's expected false-transition rate.
  \item \textbf{Hysteresis (fixed)}: hand-tuned dwell time.
  \item \textbf{Oracle}: routes with ground-truth fault and
    compute state.
\end{itemize}

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Dataset Tracks}

\textbf{Track A: TartanDrive (real off-road).} We use the full
\texttt{turnpike\_afternoon\_fall} trajectory: 1{,}198 camera
frames from \texttt{/multisense/left/image\_rect\_color} (bgr8,
$1024\times544$) in the public CMU AirLab Swift store. It appears
in two analyses. (i) \emph{Full trajectory (negative control).}
The daytime, good-visibility sequence has no frames crossing the
per-channel fault threshold (fault fraction $0.0\%$), so there is
nothing for sensor-compute coupling to exploit. (ii)
\emph{High-degradation segment.} A contiguous $309$-frame
rough-terrain segment, the opening ${\approx}31$\,s, contains real
camera degradation from bumps and occlusion spikes: fault fraction
$30.4\%$, $r=0.74$. The full trajectory and the segment are reported
separately; the segment is one contiguous window. Contention p95
latency shift is $2.50\times$ on the full trajectory and
$1.71\times$ on the segment.

\textbf{Track B: Controlled Injection (validation).}
We overlay Gaussian-PSF blur, gamma/exposure crush, and
alpha-composited occluders on real TartanDrive frames with known
onset and duration. Track B is used only to validate belief-tracker
accuracy against a ground-truth fault timeline and to run the
controlled RQ-A2 arrival experiment; no headline number depends on
it.

\textbf{Track C: WoodScape Soiling (fisheye; cross-optics
generalization).} We use the full 4{,}000-image soiling
\emph{train} split, which yields 3{,}303 frames after burst
collection. Fisheye frames are undistorted to a centered
rectilinear projection with Valeo's vendored radial camera model
before YOLO inference. The occlusion channel uses real soiling-mask
ground truth, measured as soiled-pixel fraction per frame, rather
than an optical-flow proxy; blur is computed after polar unwrap.
Fault fraction is $8.1\%$, the deadline is $341$\,ms, contention
p95 shift is $2.50\times$, and coupled $r=0.56$.

\textbf{Track D: RADIATE adverse weather (real; primary).}
We use \texttt{rain\_4\_0}, \texttt{snow\_1\_0},
\texttt{fog\_6\_0}, and \texttt{night\_1\_0}, each with
$2{,}589$--$2{,}659$ frames from the left ZED camera rectified to
\texttt{camera\_left\_rect}. RADIATE is registration-gated
(academic email, CC BY-NC-SA). Fault fraction and coupling strength
vary by weather: rain $44.4\%$ ($r=0.76$), night $12.7\%$
($r=0.68$), snow $18.5\%$ ($r=0.69$), and fog $1.9\%$ ($r=0.31$).
That spread separates frequency from severity in the results.
Contention p95 shifts are $2.54$--$2.59\times$. Faults are mostly
occlusion for rain and snow, and illumination for fog and night.
The contention schedule has a \emph{coupled} regime, where
contention follows fault onset, and an \emph{uncoupled} periodic
control independent of faults (Fig.~\ref{fig:regimes}).

\subsection{Implementation Details}

All experiments run on an Apple~M5 (10-core, 16\,GB unified memory)
using the Metal (MPS) backend in fp32; the precision axis is
CUDA-only and is not reported. Absolute latencies are specific to
this development environment, so they are not headline results. The
deadline self-calibrates per device as median nominal C1 latency
($211$--$341$\,ms here), which makes the RQ-H comparison relative to
the local frontier. Configuration is driven by
\texttt{config.yaml}; no latency-dependent threshold is hardcoded.
All comparisons use 5 seeds, 95\% $t$-interval CIs, and paired
difference tests. Seed variance comes from sensor measurement noise;
the real frames are identical across seeds.

\subsubsection{RQ-H: Joint vs.\ Decoupled (Headline)}

Table~\ref{tab:rqh} and Fig.~\ref{fig:summary} give the main
result. In the coupled regime, the joint policy lowers
deadline-miss rate in every fault-bearing condition. The reduction
is significant in five of six: the off-road segment, rain, fog,
night, and soiling. Snow is positive in point estimate, but its CI
includes zero. Track A gives both sides of the mechanism. The full
fault-free trajectory yields $0.0$\,pp, as expected for a negative
control. Its $309$-frame high-degradation segment yields
$+3.0$\,pp ($[2.49,3.51]$) at near-matched accuracy ($0.877$ vs.\
$0.880$). Every uncoupled control is also exactly $0.0$\,pp. Within
these regimes, the benefit appears only when fault--contention
coupling is imposed and disappears when the same signals are
scheduled independently. Fig.~\ref{fig:centerpiece} shows the
per-condition view for fog, the strongest case.

The effect size does not track fault frequency or correlation
strength alone. Fog has the lowest fault fraction ($1.9\%$) and the
weakest coupling ($r=0.31$), yet it has the largest benefit
($+9.4$\,pp). The likely driver is severity: rare fog
illumination/occlusion episodes make the reference config infeasible
when contention arrives, while frequent but milder rain droplets
change fewer routing decisions. This is the regime where routing
matters most: degradation is severe enough to bind the deadline.

The most severe condition also shows the tradeoff. On rain, night,
and soiling, joint and decoupled task accuracy are nearly matched
(rain: $0.855$ vs.\ $0.857$). On fog, the joint policy gives up
task accuracy ($0.861$ vs.\ $0.954$) to cut misses by $9.4$\,pp,
because it downshifts before predicted contention. We therefore
treat deadline-miss as the primary metric: in a deadline-controlled
pipeline, a late detection is often unusable downstream.

\subsubsection{Deadline-Aware Utility}

Deadline-miss rate by itself can reward aggressive downshifting, so
we also report an on-time utility:
$U = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t} a_t \,\mathbf{1}[\ell_t \le D]$. A frame
contributes its accuracy only if it meets the deadline; late frames
score zero. Unlike $\bar{a}(1{-}m)$, this keeps the chosen config's
accuracy tied to that frame's latency.
Table~\ref{tab:utility} shows nonnegative point estimates for
$\Delta U$ in all six conditions. The gains are significant for
off-road, rain, and night. Snow, fog, and soiling are positive but
not statistically distinguishable from zero. Fog is essentially
break-even ($+0.07$\,pp): the deadline-miss reduction is large, but
the accuracy cost cancels it in expected on-time accuracy.

\subsubsection{Is the Noisy-OR Necessary? A Threshold Baseline}

To test whether the noisy-OR form itself matters, we add a simple
rule-based baseline:
$p_{\text{cont}}^t = \max(b_c^t,\,\mathbf{1}[b_f^t > 0.5])$. This
hard union downshifts whenever either belief is high. As shown in
Table~\ref{tab:baseline}, the threshold rule performs similarly to
the noisy-OR joint policy on every condition. The
joint$-$threshold difference stays within $\pm0.5$\,pp and every CI
includes zero, while both coupled methods beat the decoupled
baseline by nearly the same amount. We do not claim formal
equivalence; an equivalence test with a $\pm1$\,pp margin is
under-powered at $n{=}5$. The narrower conclusion is that the soft
noisy-OR shape is not carrying the result. Feeding sensor-fault
belief into contention anticipation is.

The threshold comparison does not remove the HMMs: both coupled
policies still consume $b_f$ and $b_c$. RQ-A1 separately tests the
value of temporal belief smoothing. We also train a small
two-hidden-layer MLP ($32$--$16$ units, ReLU; cross-entropy, Adam)
from the same five inputs used by the policies: three sensor-channel
beliefs, compute belief, and deadline. Supervision comes from
per-frame oracle config labels. Each track is split temporally, with
the first $70\%$ of frames for training and the last $30\%$ held out,
and no frame shared. On held-out frames, the MLP is competitive but
inconsistent: lower deadline-miss on three tracks and higher on
four, with high variance on short single-sequence traces. We report
it as a baseline rather than a contribution. Across observation
noise levels, the joint$-$threshold gap remains within
$\pm1.2$\,pp, n.s., for noise $\sigma$ up to $0.75$ on fog and rain.

\subsubsection{Robustness: Bootstrap, Deadline, and Coupling Strength}

We run two checks beyond the primary seed CIs and the single
operating point. First, a moving-block bootstrap over time
(block $=50$ frames, $B=2000$), pooled across seeds, tests whether a
small number of frames drives the result. All six conditions exclude
zero: off-road $[0.6,4.8]$, rain $[0.3,2.3]$, snow $[1.1,4.7]$,
fog $[6.9,12.1]$, night $[3.3,8.7]$, and soiling $[1.1,2.0]$\,pp.
Snow, which is not significant under the conservative $n{=}5$ seed
interval, becomes significant under temporal resampling; we still
use the seed interval as the primary result. This check addresses
temporal structure within a sequence, not generalization across
independent sequences.

Second, sweeping the deadline on fog (Table~\ref{tab:deadline})
shows that the benefit depends on the operating point. It peaks when
the deadline binds the reference config. At tighter deadlines
($0.8$--$0.9\times$), every policy must downshift and misses vanish.
At looser deadlines ($1.1$--$1.2\times$), misses are rare and the
gap shrinks to $+0.7$\,pp (n.s.). The effect is therefore
concentrated in the borderline-feasibility regime targeted by the
median nominal C1 deadline rule. The operating-point dependence is
real; per-condition deadline sweeps remain future work.

\subsubsection{RQ-A2: Model-Derived vs.\ Fixed Dwell Time}

Under non-stationary fault arrival, model-derived hysteresis is
$\approx\!4\times$ more responsive than fixed hysteresis:
onset-to-reconfiguration lag is $1.6$ vs.\ $6.6$ frames. It also
switches $\approx\!30\%$ more often (switch rate $0.045$ vs.\
$0.034$; paired difference significant). This is a tradeoff, not a
dominance result. The right choice depends on whether
reconfiguration lag or switching cost is the active constraint.
Under stationary arrival, the two are statistically
indistinguishable, so the auto-tuned dwell matches hand tuning
without manual calibration.

\subsubsection{Belief Tracker Validation (Track B)}

On controlled injection with a known fault timeline, the blur and
occlusion belief trackers recover the injected onsets at balanced
accuracy $0.85$--$0.89$ with $3$--$6$ frame detection lag. On real
data the occlusion channel reaches balanced accuracy up to $0.95$
(e.g.\ RADIATE rain, Fig.~\ref{fig:belief}). On the fault-free
Track A all channels correctly report no sustained faults (balanced
accuracy $\approx\!0.5$, zero detected segments), consistent with
the $0.0\%$ fault fraction and the null RQ-H result.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Interpreting the Effect Size}

The strongest evidence is the switch-off behavior. The reduction is
positive on every fault-bearing condition, across three datasets and
two camera geometries, and for degradations ranging from off-road
dust to rain, snow, fog, night, and lens soiling. It is exactly zero
when we cut the correlation, and zero again on a trajectory with no
faults. A confound in the belief estimator or the metric would have
to disappear whenever only the coupling schedule changes.

Across conditions, the pattern points to severity rather than
frequency. Fog has the fewest faults and the weakest measured
correlation, yet the largest benefit, because its rare episodes are
the ones that push the reference model past the deadline. We use
``severity'' as an interpretation of the per-condition outcomes, not
as an independently measured variable; a controlled severity sweep
would be needed to isolate it. The threshold baseline in
Table~\ref{tab:baseline} supports the same reading. A hand-set rule
on the sensor belief performs about as well as the probabilistic
version, so the result comes from coupling the beliefs, not from the
noisy-OR form in particular.

\subsection{When Persistence Helps and Hurts}

Persistence-aware belief improves detection balanced accuracy on
all fault-bearing tracks and reduces config switching for slow,
sustained degradations: fog, night, and soiling. It passes the full
pre-specified gate on night. It hurts on rain, where high-frequency
droplet occlusion violates the slow-onset assumption in the HMM
smoothing. The practical lesson is not to discard persistence, but
to match the smoothing time constant to the fault's temporal scale.

\subsection{Limitations}

The evaluation is offline and trace-driven, with no
hardware-in-the-loop. Compute contention is non-thermal commodity
load represented by a two-state model; embedded contention can have
richer structure. The sensor-to-compute coupling is imposed by the
coupled/uncoupled regimes rather than measured from natural
co-occurrence. That design supports the causal reading, because the
uncoupled arm is the falsification test, but it does not prove that
field robots exhibit the same coupling. The pseudo-GT is
agreement-with-reference and is blind to systematic C1 errors. The
per-channel labels used to fit the sensor HMM come from the same
health signals that the belief later filters, so detection
validation is partly internal; Track~B's controlled injection, with
independent onset labels, reduces but does not eliminate that
circularity. HMM calibration is shared across all policies, so the
RQ-H delta is not biased by policy-specific calibration, but
absolute belief calibration is optimistic. RADIATE uses only the
rectified left camera; radar, LiDAR, and detection annotations are
unused. WoodScape soiling order is 7--8 frame bursts, not
continuous video time. Finally, the five seeds reuse the same frames
and vary injected sensor-measurement noise, so the CIs measure
sensitivity to that noise rather than generalization across
sequences, vehicles, or contention traces. The temporal-block
bootstrap checks within-sequence resampling, but cross-environment
intervals require independent trajectories. Absolute latencies are
specific to Apple~M5/MPS and are not headline results.

\subsection{Future Work}

Three directions are immediate. \emph{(i) Full joint posterior.}
Replace the noisy-OR coupling with a factorial or cross-coupled HMM
that maintains $P(z_f^t, z_c^t \mid o_{1:t})$, moving from coupled
belief fusion to joint belief-state estimation. \emph{(ii) Learned
baselines.} The decoupled ablation, threshold rule
(Table~\ref{tab:baseline}), and small MLP router
(\S\ref{sec:experiments}) are useful but not exhaustive. A tuned
contextual bandit or learned router over $(b_f, b_c)$, plus an
oracle with access to the coupling schedule, would better bound the
value of learned fusion. \emph{(iii) Generalization and realism.}
Contention intensity, fault--contention delay, and HMM
window/transition parameters should be swept as we swept deadline
and $\kappa$. The larger step is independent trajectories for
cross-environment CIs, reportable datacenter-GPU latencies,
embedded contention on a Jetson, measured natural co-occurrence,
RADIATE detection annotations instead of pseudo-GT, and a closed-loop
downstream task such as MuJoCo navigation with rendered fault
injection.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We built a deadline-aware perception router that estimates
sensor-fault belief and compute-contention belief with separate HMMs
and couples them at decision time. Across three real datasets and
two camera geometries, coupling reduced deadline misses by $1.1$ to
$9.4$ points when the two failures were correlated. The gain was
zero in every matched uncoupled control and zero on a fault-free
trajectory. The benefit appears to track how severely a degradation
strains the deadline, not just how often faults occur, and a simple
threshold on sensor belief matched the noisy-OR policy under our
conditions. Temporal smoothing helped slow degradations but hurt
fast-flickering rain. The coupling here is imposed by design; the
next test is whether the same structure appears in fielded systems
with naturally co-occurring faults and compute contention.
